[Life] Japanese conversation three ends

At first, I decided to take this course on a whim, wanting to take more Japanese classes to earn credits and practice speaking. As a result, taking this course not only made me realize the importance of intonation, but also helped me prepare for pre-reading articles and even benefited my N1 exam.

Class mode

The class format is similar to what I saw on Bahamut previously. Almost all activities are conducted in Japanese, but when the teacher notices that you don’t understand, they will switch back to Chinese. The teacher will first distribute the teaching materials to the students, then we will read them on our own and fill out the forms provided by the teacher, and submit them before the deadline. The teacher will print out and “personally” correct each form filled out by the students, and send them back to everyone a few weeks later. Although there are only about 15 students in the class, I still admire the teacher’s perseverance.

Afterwards, the teacher will read the article together with the class and have each person take turns reading the article. Then the teacher will ask about the main points of the paragraph or the meaning of certain words. After finishing the article reading, the teacher will open the response form for everyone to discuss the answers. The general class format is as described above. We have read a total of 3 articles this semester.

In addition, there are also midterm and final presentations, usually choosing one topic out of three to present, with a time limit of 5 minutes, and a question and answer session at the end. Although everyone seems to be nervous, each presentation still surprises us with the diverse talents of our classmates XD.

Note

The most rewarding part of this course for me has been the notes. Initially, I only intended to take notes for my own class records and homework, but unconsciously I found myself gaining more insights as I continued to take notes, and I began to want to share them with others. Additionally, I noticed that the teacher initially used Word to teach class, adding pronunciation to several important words using the built-in function. However, I observed that teaching in this manner could lead to issues due to variations in students’ proficiency levels. Some students might struggle to pronounce relatively simple words without markings, and others may have difficulty mastering the tones. As a result, even I felt that there were some issues with the pronunciation. Therefore, I started to consider the possibility of using my notes as teaching materials for everyone.

So after a day of classes, I proposed to the teacher whether to use my notes as teaching materials, share them with everyone to read, and use them as projected materials to help with the class. At first, I was nervous and hesitant, afraid that my classmates would think I was presumptuous or overbearing. However, I didn’t expect the teacher to readily accept my proposal, and after using the notes I provided for class, some classmates even came to me to say they thought I did a great job. Hearing their feedback made me really happy! I feel that my efforts have paid off, which is really great.

Next semester, I also plan to continue taking the teacher’s Japanese conversation class four, and I have also found a friend to practice Japanese with. I think I will definitely continue to take these notes, and I also plan to take it a step further to make these notes even more perfect! I hope that after these efforts, the finished product will surprise the teacher and classmates.

Regarding technical content about note-taking, I plan to write another article to record it later. Please come and take a look at it then!

The final lesson with Tomo-san

As for the last week of the course, the teacher invited a Japanese person, Tomo-san, to listen to our final presentations and chat with us about the Japanese job market. I found it quite interesting and it made me even more curious about what the working environment in Japan, which has always been described as full of “black-hearted companies,” is really like. This can also be considered one of the purposes of my intention to go on exchange this time.

Finally, I enjoyed my first wagashi and personally prepared matcha. I’m not sure if it’s because it was my first time using a tea whisk, but I didn’t make a lot of foam and even got a not-so-flattering picture taken by the teacher 😢. However, I personally didn’t feel a strong bitter taste. On the contrary, the green tea the teacher prepared had a salty taste that I’ve never experienced in Taiwan, which was quite surprising.

Conclusion

This semester, taking this course not only allowed me to meet another group of friends who can go sing in karaoke together, but also introduced me to another impressive Japanese teacher. This course not only trained the four key points of listening, speaking, reading, and writing, but also deepened my understanding of the importance of intonation. Because my ears were already accustomed to hearing the correct pronunciation, I often unconsciously mutter to myself in my mind when others say something wrong XD. But when it comes to expressing myself, I often make a lot of mistakes by not thinking too much. So these experiences also made me more aware of the details of pronunciation when speaking. All in all, it was really great to take this course this semester. Looking forward to the next semester and taking classes with new friends!
